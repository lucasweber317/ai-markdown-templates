# Personas

**Instructions:** Create 1-3 primary personas representing your key user types. Each persona should capture role, context, goals, pain points, and success criteria. Use real research and customer insights—avoid generic assumptions.

---

## Persona: [Persona Name]

**Instructions:** Give this persona a descriptive name based on their role or primary characteristic (e.g., "Engineering Team Lead," "Healthcare Administrator," "Sales Operations Manager")

### Role & Context
**Instructions:** Describe who this person is within their organization and their day-to-day reality.

- **Job title:** Their formal role(s) (can be multiple similar titles)
- **Department:** Which part of the organization they belong to
- **Organization type:** Type and size of company they work for
- **Typical team size:** How many people they work with or manage
- **Decision authority:** Are they a buyer, user, influencer, or champion? Can they sign contracts?

### Firmographic
**Instructions:** Describe the type of company this persona works for. This helps with market segmentation and prioritization.

- Company size and employee count
- Industry verticals or sectors most common
- Technology maturity or digital sophistication level
- Geographic distribution (if relevant)
- Typical tech stack or adjacent tools they use

### Goals & Jobs-To-Be-Done
**Instructions:** What is this persona trying to accomplish? Focus on outcomes, not activities.

- **Primary goals:** The main objectives they're accountable for in their role
- **Secondary goals:** Important but not critical objectives
- **Key outcomes they are accountable for:** What does success look like for this person? What do they get measured on?

### Pain Points
**Instructions:** List the specific problems and frustrations this persona experiences. Be concrete—avoid generic statements like "wants to save time." These should connect to problems your product solves.

Example format:
- Spending 10+ hours per week manually consolidating reports from multiple systems
- Unable to get real-time visibility into project status, leading to last-minute surprises
- Team members miss critical deadlines because notifications are scattered across tools
- Lack of standardization across teams creates inconsistent data quality

### Behavioral Traits
**Instructions:** Describe how this persona approaches decisions, change, and technology. This informs how you position and sell to them.

- **Buying behavior:** Are they early adopters or late majority? Do they evaluate 10 options or go with trusted recommendations?
- **Risk tolerance:** Do they prefer proven, established solutions or are they willing to try new approaches?
- **Change adoption style:** How do they approach organizational change—cautiously, enthusiastically, pragmatically?
- **Tooling maturity level:** Do they prefer simple, easy-to-use tools or powerful, complex solutions with steep learning curves?

### Constraints
**Instructions:** What limitations or requirements does this persona operate within? These inform go-to-market and product decisions.

- **Budget constraints:** How do they secure budget? What's their spending authority?
- **Compliance or regulatory requirements:** Industry regulations, certifications, or standards they must adhere to
- **Technical constraints:** Integration requirements, infrastructure limitations, data residency needs, security requirements

### Success Criteria
**Instructions:** How does this persona evaluate whether a solution is working? What metrics or outcomes matter to them?

- **How they define success:** Qualitative outcomes that matter (e.g., "my team is more productive," "we pass audits easily," "no one complains about the tool")
- **Metrics they care about:** Quantitative measures (e.g., time saved, error reduction, adoption rate, ROI, compliance score)
